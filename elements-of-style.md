# The Elements of Style — Principle Reference

*Distilled from William Strunk Jr., The Elements of Style (1918). Public domain.*

---

## I. Elementary Rules of Usage

**Rule 1: Form the possessive singular of nouns by adding 's**
- Follow this regardless of final consonant: *Charles's friend*, *Burns's poems*, *the witch's malice*.
- Exceptions: ancient proper names ending in -es/-is (use "the heel of Achilles,"), the possessive *Jesus'*, and phrases like "for conscience' sake."
- Pronominal possessives (*hers*, *its*, *theirs*, *yours*, *oneself*) take no apostrophe.

**Rule 2: In a series of three or more terms with a single conjunction, use a comma after each term except the last**
- *red, white, and blue* — *gold, silver, or copper*

**Rule 3: Enclose parenthetic expressions between commas**
- Never insert one comma of a pair while omitting the other.
- Non-restrictive (supplementary) clauses are parenthetic and need commas; restrictive (identifying) clauses do not.
- Wrong: "Marjorie's husband, Colonel Nelson paid us a visit yesterday."
- Right: "Marjorie's husband, Colonel Nelson, paid us a visit yesterday."

**Rule 4: Place a comma before a conjunction introducing a co-ordinate clause**
- Applies to *and*, *but*, *or*, *nor*, *for*, *while* (meaning "at the same time"), and *as* (meaning "because").
- If the second clause begins with an adverb, use a semicolon instead.
- Omit the comma when both clauses share a subject and the relation is close.

**Rule 5: Do not join independent clauses by a comma or an em dash**
- Use semicolons between grammatically complete clauses not joined by a conjunction.
- Before: "Stevenson's romances are entertaining, they are full of exciting adventures."
- After: "Stevenson's romances are entertaining; they are full of exciting adventures."
- Exception: very short, similarly formed clauses without cause-and-consequence relationships may use commas.

**Rule 6: Do not break sentences in two**
- Do not use periods for commas.
- Before: "I met them on a Cunard liner several years ago. Coming home from Liverpool to New York."
- After: "I met them on a Cunard liner several years ago, coming home from Liverpool to New York."

**Rule 7: A participial phrase at the beginning of a sentence must refer to the grammatical subject**
- Before: "Being in a dilapidated condition, I was able to buy the house very cheap."
- Recast so the subject matches the participle — the house is dilapidated, not *I*.

## II. Elementary Principles of Composition

**Rule 8: Make the paragraph the unit of composition — one paragraph to each topic**
- Each new paragraph signals a new step in the development.
- Avoid single-sentence paragraphs except in dialogue or brief transitions.

**Rule 9: Begin each paragraph with a topic sentence; end it in conformity with the beginning**
- The reader should discover the purpose at once and retain it through the conclusion.
- Pattern: (a) topic sentence, (b) sentences that explain or develop it, (c) a closing sentence that emphasizes the thought or states a consequence.
- Avoid ending with a digression or unimportant detail.

**Rule 10: Use the active voice**
- The active voice is usually more direct and vigorous than the passive.
- Before: "There were a great number of dead leaves lying on the ground."
- After: "Dead leaves covered the ground."
- The passive is acceptable when the subject is the receiver of the action or when it reads more naturally. Do not make one passive depend on another.

**Rule 11: Put statements in positive form**
- Make definite assertions. Avoid tame, colorless, hesitating, non-committal language.
- Use *not* for denial or antithesis, never for evasion.
- Before: "He was not very often on time." → After: "He usually came late."
- Negative and positive together create strong antithesis: "Not charity, but simple justice."

**Rule 12: Use definite, specific, concrete language**
- Prefer the specific to the general, the definite to the vague, the concrete to the abstract.
- Before: "A period of unfavorable weather set in." → After: "It rained every day for a week."
- Even when discussing general principles, furnish particular instances.

**Rule 13: Omit needless words**
- Vigorous writing is concise. A sentence should contain no unnecessary words, a paragraph no unnecessary sentences, for the same reason that a drawing should have no unnecessary lines and a machine no unnecessary parts.
- This requires not that every sentence be short, but that every word tell.
- Revise out *the fact that* wherever it occurs.
- Before (51 words): "Macbeth was very ambitious. This led him to wish to become king of Scotland. The witches told him that this wish of his would come true. The king of Scotland at this time was Duncan. Encouraged by his wife, Macbeth murdered Duncan. He was thus enabled to succeed Duncan as king."
- After (26 words): "Encouraged by his wife, Macbeth achieved his ambition and realized the prediction of the witches by murdering Duncan and becoming king of Scotland in his place."

**Rule 14: Avoid a succession of loose sentences**
- Do not build whole paragraphs from sentences of two co-ordinate clauses joined by a conjunction or relative.
- A single such sentence is fine; a series becomes monotonous.
- Vary with simple sentences, semicolons, periodic sentences, or three-clause constructions.

**Rule 15: Express co-ordinate ideas in similar form**
- Similar content and function should be outwardly similar in construction.
- Before: "Formerly, science was taught by the textbook method, while now the laboratory method is employed."
- After: "Formerly, science was taught by the textbook method; now it is taught by the laboratory method."
- Correlatives (*both…and*, *not…but*, *not only…but also*, *either…or*) must be followed by the same grammatical construction.

**Rule 16: Keep related words together**
- The position of words shows their relationship. Bring related words together; keep unrelated words apart.
- Do not separate subject and verb with a transferable phrase.
- Place the relative pronoun immediately after its antecedent.
- Before: "He wrote three articles about his adventures in Spain, which were published in Harper's Magazine."
- After: "He published in Harper's Magazine three articles about his adventures in Spain."

**Rule 17: In summaries, keep to one tense**
- Use present tense throughout when summarizing a drama, poem, story, or novel. Whichever tense is chosen, use it consistently.
- Shifting tenses gives an appearance of uncertainty.

**Rule 18: Place the emphatic words of a sentence at the end**
- The end of a sentence is the position of greatest prominence.
- Before: "Humanity has hardly advanced in fortitude since that time, though it has advanced in many other ways."
- After: "Humanity, since that time, has advanced in many other ways, but it has hardly advanced in fortitude."
- The other prominent position is the beginning. This principle applies equally to sentences within a paragraph and paragraphs within a composition.

## III. A Few Matters of Form

- Write dates and serial numbers as figures, not words: *August 9, 1918*; *Chapter XII*.
- Punctuate around parentheses as if the parenthetical expression were absent.
- Introduce formal documentary quotations with a colon; use a comma before quotations in apposition or serving as direct objects.
- Indirect discourse (introduced by *that*) takes no quotation marks.
- Proverbial and familiar literary phrases require no quotation marks.
- Italicize titles of literary works and capitalize important words.

## IV. Words and Expressions Commonly Misused

| Use this                  | Not this                      |
| ------------------------- | ----------------------------- |
| *whether*                 | *as to whether*               |
| *reliable*, *trustworthy* | *dependable*                  |
| *different from*          | *different than*              |
| *owing to*, *because of*  | *due to* (adverbially)        |
| *often*                   | *oftentimes*, *ofttimes*      |
| *almost*                  | *most* (as adverb)            |
| *students*                | *student body*                |
| *point of view*           | *viewpoint*                   |
| *fewer* (number)          | *less* (for countable things) |

**Words to use precisely:**
- **Can** means ability; **may** means permission.
- **Claim** means lay claim to; not a synonym for *declare* or *maintain*.
- **Compare to** for different orders; **compare with** for same order.
- **Consider** is not followed by *as* when meaning "believe to be."
- **Data** is plural: "These data were tabulated."
- **Effect** (noun: result; verb: bring about) vs. **affect** (verb: influence).
- **Fact** — only for directly verifiable matters, not judgments.
- **Like** governs nouns; **as** introduces clauses.
- **Shall** (first person) vs. **will** (second/third) for simple future.
- **State** means express fully; not a substitute for *say* or *remark*.
- **While** — restrict to "during the time that"; avoid using for *and*, *but*, or *although*.

**Words to avoid or cut:**
- **Certainly**, **very**, **interesting**, **clever** — overused intensifiers or vague praise. Use sparingly.
- **Case**, **character**, **nature**, **system**, **factor**, **feature** — usually filler. Cut or replace with direct phrasing.
- **He is a man who** — redundant. "He is a man who is very ambitious" → "He is very ambitious."
- **The fact that** — revise out of every sentence.
- **Line, along these lines** — replace with direct phrasing.
- **Literal, literally** — do not use to prop up exaggeration.
- **Lose out**, **try out**, **win out**, **sign up** — no more emphatic than the simple verb.
- **So** — avoid as an intensifier in writing.
- **Thanking you in advance** — presumptuous; use "Will you please" or "I shall be obliged."

**Other guidance:**
- **All right** — always two words.
- **But** — unnecessary after *doubt* and *help*.
- **However** meaning *nevertheless* — do not place first; put it later in the sentence.
- **Possessive before gerund**: "Do you mind *my* asking?" not "me asking."
- **Respective/respectively** — usually omit.
- **Split infinitive** — avoid when possible.
- **Worth while** — applies to actions only, not nouns.

## V. Spelling

- A single consonant preceded by a stressed short vowel doubles before *-ed* and *-ing*: *planned*, *letting*, *beginning*. (*Coming* is an exception.)
- Write *any one*, *every one*, *some one*, *some time* as two words when emphasis falls on each word individually.
